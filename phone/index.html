About Wirecutter

Gadget shopping can be difficult at times. But I think I can help.

Pick the category of gadgets that you want to shop in, and then read the descriptions of our handful of choices. Each one is special, but I bet there's one that fits you. We've gone with high end, low end, and what we believe is just right.

I study materials put forth by gadget companies. And I read the forums and user reviews on sites like Amazon and GDGT. One thing I've learned over the years as an assigning editor is who the talent is in the gadget review game. I talk to the best journalists who review gadgets like John Falcone and his team at CNET for home theater gear (excepting projectors), or Richard Baguley at Reviewed.com about cameras. Or Ars Technica when it comes to new operating systems. Or Sascha Segan when it comes to mobile networks. There are dozens of really great tech reporters, and I know them and who is best at what.

I take all the data I can find from their work and from around the web. And then I make up my mind. Sometimes I agree with the people who have tested things. But sometimes I recommend a lower or higher tiered gadget depending on what I think most people will need.

I've been writing about gadgets for a few years now. I was at Gizmodo for half a decade (yikes!) and Wired magazine before that. And before that, I worked at a little computer magazine called Maximum PC. Also, my father was an engineer for HP and instead of teaching me how to play baseball, at the age of 7 he taught me how to solder. We built remote controlled cars together. For better or worse, gadgets are a big part of who I am. But on the other hand, gadgets are not what I am all about. I'm proud to say both. You shouldn't trust anyone for your gadget advice who doesn't have a life inside and outside gadgets. Context is important.

When I say best, I don't mean the one that costs the most or has the most features. In many cases, the top of the line models are unnecessary for most people.

It's true that one of the best ways to find gadgets is to do it by sale. But there are plenty of really amazing gadgets at good prices, in case thats the way you want to go. I rarely will recommend the most expensive thing, however. Because we live in an age where most gadgets are good enough, I generally like to find the sweet spot of cost as balanced out by the features we really need. And pick a model above and below that exact point.

A GREAT PROJECTOR

Panasonic PT-AE7000U

Key Specs

Resolution:

1920 x 1080

Contrast:

300,000:1

Brightness:

2,000 lumens

Lens Shift:

horizontal and

vertical (manual)

Buy On Amazon

Panasonic's PT-AE7000U is remarkable. The company collaborates with professional cinematographers to fine tune its home theater projectors.

Read More

Since its release in October, the PT-AE7000U has garnered rave reviews for its outstanding picture quality, including excellent black levels and color performance. In fact, Projector Central says the 2D performance alone rivals or beats projectors that are triple the price. Art Feierman at Projector Reviews also notes that the majority of the AE7000U's competition–the Sony VPL-HW30ES, the Epson Home Cinema 5010, the Optoma HD8300, Sharp XV-Z17000–are mostly a full price step up from the Panasonic, and that none of them come close to hitting the same performance/price sweet spot.

While not cheap (it's selling for around $3,000 right now), this projector is still competitively priced considering the features you get. These include 3 HDMI 1.4a inputs, power zoom and focus, lots of manual lens shift, tons of picture tweaking tools, and a 240Hz image in 3D mode for eliminating crosstalk. At 2,000 lumens, the AE7000U is also close to 25 percent brighter than most similarly-priced 1080p 3D home theater projectors. That makes a huge difference with 3D movies as glasses significantly dim the picture. (Just remember, as with any other projector, ambient light can, and will, ruin an otherwise great picture.) The projector will even let you adjust the depth of the 3D image and is capable of converting 2D content to 3D in real time, although in my experience, this is never really that impressive.

Of course, the selection of quality 3D movies is still extremely limited, so the fact that 2D performance alone rivals projectors that cost twice as much is huge plus for this projector. If you want just a higher end 2D projector though, you can also go with the Panasonic AE4000U, which is a little over $2000. Like the 7000, it has plenty of calibration options and a fantastic picture. Admittedly, the AE7000U is still a relatively new projector so we'll continue to look at its competition and monitor reviews as they come in.
